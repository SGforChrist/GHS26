Singapore · General Household Survey 2025

Singapore's churches didn't start losing young people in 2025.
They started in 2015.

The Department of Statistics confirmed the headline number last month: the first recorded drop in Protestant Christianity's share of the population in four decades. But that's not when this began. Tracked the right way — the same people, followed as they age, not different people compared at different times — the decline among the young shows up a full five years earlier. This page walks through why that distinction matters, using only figures independently checked against Singapore's own published data.

Part I — What the headline says

The number that made the news

On 30 June 2026, SingStat published the General Household Survey 2025. It confirmed what quieter data had been hinting at for a decade: Christianity's share of Singapore's resident population fell for the first time in forty years of records. Protestant Christianity specifically — SingStat's own category is "Christianity – Other Christians" — fell from 11.9% of residents in 2020 to 9.5% in 2025.

One distinction worth holding onto before anything else: Catholicism grew over the same period. This is not Singapore turning secular across the board — it's a specific model of Protestant practice losing people, while religious non-affiliation also rises. That's a narrower, more specific claim, and it's the one the rest of this page is actually about.

Part II — What the headline doesn't show

Two ways to ask the same question — and one of them was hiding the answer

Here is the methodological point this whole page rests on, explained without jargon. Every news story about the 2025 numbers compares different people at different times — the 15-to-19-year-olds surveyed in 2020 against a different set of 15-to-19-year-olds surveyed in 2025. That comparison is real, but it can hide exactly when a trend started. A stricter test follows the same people: take the cohort that was 15–19 in 2015, and ask what became of that specific group of people once survey-takers found them again in 2020, now aged 20–24.

Each survey year, snapshotted separately. Because a different set of people is measured each time, a slow-building, age-specific shift can look like noise.

2010 → 2015

Every one of the five tracked transitions — from the teenage years into the early forties — was positive. Each cohort gained ground as it aged, the normal pattern for religious identification.

2015 → 2020

Every transition weakened. But only two flipped into outright decline: 15–19 into 20–24 (−0.90pp) and 20–24 into 25–29 (−0.43pp). The three older transitions stayed positive — smaller, but still growing. This is a youth-specific, age-graded pattern, not a broad shift. It is five years before the 2025 headline, and it shows up only among the young.

2020 → 2025

Every transition turned negative — but now uniformly, all falling within a narrow 1.9–2.8 percentage-point band regardless of age. That's the signature of a broad, shared shock hitting everyone at once. It is real, and it's reported in full below. But on this evidence it is not, by itself, a story about youth.

This is the actual case against "more of the same." A uniform, all-ages shock in 2020–2025 is one kind of event. A gradient that appears five years earlier and only among the young is a different kind of event, with a different cause — and it was measurable in the data in real time, years before anyone wrote a headline about it.

Part III — The longer view

A generation's momentum was always going to run out eventually

Many of Singapore's largest congregations trace their growth to the 1970s and 80s revival era — the 1978 Billy Graham crusade alone drew close to twelve thousand professions of faith in five evenings. That generation, and its children, kept the church skewing youthful for decades. But that was always a store of momentum being drawn down, not a permanent feature.

Tracking single birth cohorts across their own life course shows the drawdown directly. The cohort that was 15–19 in 2000 gained ground for twenty full years before its eventual reversal. The cohort that was 15–19 in 2010 gained for only five years before flattening. The cohort that was 15–19 in 2015 shows no growth phase at all — decline from the first re-measurement. Fifteen-to-19-year-olds measured in 2025 sit at roughly the same point the very first cohort in this dataset started from a quarter-century earlier, after that first cohort had already built up, and then largely given back, everything it gained.

Today's most engaged young adults are the small-group leaders and parents of 2040. A shrinking, ageing base of young engagement doesn't just describe today — it substantially shapes the size of the problem two decades out.

The 2020–2025 shock, by age — and why graduates matter

There's no clean age gradient in the most recent five-year shock — it hit middle-aged and elderly residents as hard as the young. Reported in full, because a page arguing for precision about the youth-specific pattern above shouldn't cherry-pick the numbers that fit it.

One more cut is harder to wave away. Among university graduates, the Christian share fell from 32.2% (2010) to 28.3% (2020) to 24.9% (2025) — the steepest decline of any education band tracked. If this were simply about production values or relevance, the most-educated wouldn't be leaving fastest. It points toward a substance-and-credibility question rather than a style one.

Part IV — In brief: why

The data says what. The full paper argues why.

This page exists to make the numbers legible and checkable. The full paper goes further, tracing a five-part diagnosis — theological substance, cultural formation, leadership dynamics, structural design, and wider social pressure — and argues these aren't five separate problems so much as five places the same underlying pattern shows up. Three findings from that argument, briefly:

01

Inherited pressure, not chosen faith

In Singapore's own generational research, 36% of Millennial and Gen Z church members strongly agreed — and a further 40% moderately agreed — that being expected to stay in a parent's choice of church is a major reason young Christians disengage.

02

A standard of proof this generation applies everywhere else

Formed by a STEM-heavy education system and algorithmically-checkable information, this generation is documented expecting demonstrated reasoning before extending trust — not unfamiliarity with vocabulary, but a rigor bar many congregations aren't built to meet.

03

Scale predicts outflow better than tradition does

Singapore's own cross-institutional church research (SOTC 2022) found congregations over 2,000 members had more than double the outflow rate of smaller ones — and accounted for 73% of all outflow nationally. Denomination wasn't the strongest predictor. Size and structure were.

The full paper traces the causal connections between these factors rather than listing them side by side, and spends its second half on what individual congregations — and Singapore's churches together — can actually do about it. That's in the PDF, not repeated here.

Part V — If you're wondering what to actually do

What this page is, and isn't, asking of you

This isn't a campaign, and it doesn't have a membership list. It exists because the data is a decade older than most people realise, and because "more of the same" is a genuinely different claim from what the numbers show. What happens next isn't up to a website — it's up to whoever's actually positioned to act on it. A few honest, calibrated suggestions, depending on who you are:

If you attend a church

The single most useful thing you can do with this page is put it — or the paper — in front of someone who leads a congregation or a ministry. This is a data problem before it's an activism problem; most leaders haven't seen the cohort-level breakdown, because until now it required a special data request to build.

If you lead a ministry or small group

The paper's practical section (Part IV) starts with a concrete, low-cost first step: take the young people who came through your youth ministry five, ten, fifteen years ago, and find out — by name — how many are still meaningfully engaged anywhere. Most congregations have this data already; almost none have looked at it this way.

If you're a researcher, journalist, or in SOTC's network

The full cohort-transition methodology, and the underlying SingStat tables, are laid out in the paper. It's built to be checked, extended, and argued with — not taken on faith.

Every figure on this page was independently recomputed from SingStat's own published age-band tables (Census of Population 2000/2010/2020; General Household Survey 2015/2025), not copied from any secondary source. Where this page's calculations are original synthesis — the cohort-transition analysis in particular — that's stated plainly rather than presented as an official SingStat product; SingStat publishes the age-band tables, the cohort-tracking is this analysis applied to them.
