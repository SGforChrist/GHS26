First, what made the news

The number everyone's already seen

On 30 June 2026, SingStat published the General Household Survey 2025. It confirmed what quieter data had been hinting at for a decade: Christianity's share of Singapore's resident population fell for the first time in forty years of records. Protestant Christianity specifically — SingStat's own category is "Christianity – Other Christians" — fell from 11.9% of residents in 2020 to 9.5% in 2025.

Worth holding onto before anything else: Catholicism grew over the same period. This isn't Singapore turning secular across the board — it's a specific model of Protestant practice losing people. Everything below is about that narrower, more specific claim.

Before the chart, the idea behind it

Two different questions that sound like the same question

Picture a school photographing its Primary 1 class every year. Comparing this year's photo to last year's tells you something about Primary 1 in general — but it can't tell you what happened to any specific child, because it's a different set of children each time. Now picture instead following the same forty children from Primary 1 through Primary 6. That second method is slower to set up. It's also the only one that can tell you exactly when something changed, for whom, and how fast.

Nearly every news story about the 2025 numbers does the first thing: compare different people at different times. That's real data, but it can hide exactly when a trend started. The chart below does the second thing: it follows the same people — the cohort that was 15–19 in 2015, re-measured five years later at 20–24 in 2020 — and asks not just whether that group's numbers moved, but when they first started moving.

Each survey year, snapshotted separately. Because a different set of people is measured each time, a slow-building, age-specific shift can look like noise.

2010 → 2015

Every one of the five tracked transitions — teenage years into the early forties — was positive. Normal pattern: religious identification rising as people age.

2015 → 2020

Only two transitions flipped negative: 15–19 into 20–24 (−0.90pp) and 20–24 into 25–29 (−0.43pp). The three older transitions stayed positive. Five years before the 2025 headline, and only among the young.

2020 → 2025

Every transition went negative — uniformly, 1.9–2.8pp regardless of age. The signature of a broad shock hitting everyone at once, reported in full below, but not on this evidence a story specifically about youth.

That's the real case against "more of the same." A uniform shock in 2020–2025 is one kind of event. A gradient that appears five years earlier, only among the young, is a different kind of event — and it was measurable years before anyone wrote a headline about it.

Pushing the question back further

If 2015 is when it turned negative — was there a signal even before that?

This is worth being precise about, because it's tempting to look for a clean story and this data doesn't quite hand you one. Two things hold up. One thing doesn't, and it's reported here anyway.

Holds up

The 15–19 band's growth had already stalled

Extrapolate the pace of growth from 2000–2010 forward five more years, and the 15–19 age band should have gained roughly 2.13 percentage points by 2015. It actually gained 0.29 — about 14% of trend. Still technically growing. Nowhere near the pace its own recent past would have predicted.

This is specific to the 15–19 band. The 20–24 band does not show this — it grew faster than its own trend implied over the same window. This isn't a story about "the young" broadly stalling in 2010–2015. It's a story about the very youngest band specifically.

Holds up

The peak moved later, in order, by age

15–19 and 20–24 both peaked by 2015 and have declined at every survey since. 25–29 kept climbing until 2020. The front of the reversal crossed the age bands in sequence — it didn't arrive everywhere at once, even years before 2025.

Tested — doesn't hold up

"The youngest transitions were already the weakest in 2010–2015"

Checked this directly against the transition data before writing anything: it isn't clean. The 30–34→35–39 transition grew just as slowly (+2.1% relative) as the youngest two that window. There's no clear age gradient in the 2010–2015 transition sizes themselves — only in the cross-sectional signals above. Reported here rather than quietly dropped, because a page arguing for precision shouldn't cherry-pick the tests that cooperate.

Put together: this isn't a story that starts in 2025, or even cleanly in 2015. The clearest read is that a specific, narrow slowdown was visible in the data by 2010–2015 for the youngest band, the front of the reversal moved through the age bands over the following decade, and by 2015–2020 it had become a defined, youth-specific pattern — a full five years before it showed up as a national headline.

The whole shape, together

Five birth cohorts, each followed across its own life course, 2000–2025. Cohorts whose growth phase already ended or reversed before 2020. Earlier cohorts. Hover a point.

For completeness, briefly

The 2020–2025 shock hit every age — and graduates hardest

No clean age gradient in the most recent five-year shock — it hit middle-aged and elderly residents as hard as the young. In full, because a page arguing for precision shouldn't only show the numbers that fit its own case.

One cut is harder to wave away: among university graduates, the Christian share fell from 32.2% (2010) to 28.3% (2020) to 24.9% (2025) — the steepest decline of any education band. If this were about production values or relevance, the most-educated wouldn't be leaving fastest. Points toward substance and credibility, not style.

The data says what. This is where the paper explains why.

Five things happening at once — and the paper argues they're connected

This page exists to make the numbers legible and checkable. The full paper goes further: it traces the causal links between five factors, and argues they're not five separate problems so much as five places the same underlying pattern shows up. Five findings, briefly — the connective argument is in the PDF, not repeated here.

Theology

A faith not built to survive being tested

Sociologist Christian Smith's "Moralistic Therapeutic Deism" — a folk faith where God mainly exists to make people feel good, with little reference to sin or repentance. When life delivers a real test, what collapses isn't a tested faith. It's one that was never grounded to begin with.

Culture

Inherited pressure, not chosen faith

36% of Millennial and Gen Z church members strongly agreed — a further 40% moderately agreed — that being expected to stay in a parent's choice of church is a major reason young Christians disengage.

Leadership

A two-way failure to trust the other generation

Older leaders often hold documented, unflattering views of younger members' readiness that justify retaining control. Younger members, in turn, describe leaving rather than confronting it — Gen X was the generation most likely to have already left their original church.

Structure

Silos that split families under one roof

One elder, describing his own family: he doesn't see his wife and children — all in the same building — until they reconvene for the drive home. Three members of one household, effectively attending three different churches.

Scale

Size predicts outflow better than tradition

SOTC 2022 found congregations over 2,000 members had more than double the outflow rate of smaller ones, accounting for 73% of all outflow nationally. Denomination wasn't the strongest predictor. Size and structure were.

The paper's actual argument is that these five aren't a list — they're one pattern (a performance-and-evaluation logic borrowed from Singapore's own meritocracy) showing up in five different places. That argument, and what individual congregations can do about it, is the second half of the PDF.
